Structure of the paper
======================

0. Abstract
-----------
It's an abstract, should be the last thing we write.


1. Introduction
---------------
How I would structure the paragraphs:

- Curry-Howard isomorphism spiel borrowed from the paper

- SAX: Present SAX in one paragraph, how it arises from G3 by replacing 
  noninvertible rules with axioms and invertible rules are left unchanged. Maybe
  plug in one sentence about the computational interpretation.

- Overview of the proof theory: Resulting proof system proves the same sequents,
  but the proof theory is more subtle: explain that standard cut-elimination
  fails, explain why, and how it's replaced by a modified form of
  cut-elimination where certain analytic cuts (snips) are permitted to remain in
  normal-form proofs while preserving the subformula property.

- Mechanizing the proof theory: proofs of cut admissibility and of the
  subformula property are more involved because we need to track which formula
  occurences are eligible for a snip and which are irrelevant. Explain the
  structure of the proofs and why this makes it an interesting target for
  mechanization. 

- Contributions: "In this paper, we report on a mechanization of the proof 
  theory of SAX in Beluga, ..." Description of Beluga. Explain why Beluga is (or
  isn't) a good fit for this project. State our main contributions more
  explicitly. "We also describe the challenges encountered in ...". End with the
  classic "This is to the best of our knowledge the first mechanization of SAX's
  proof theory...". Also make it clear that we mechanize a representative subset
  of SAX.

- Structure of the paper: "The remainder of the paper is organized as follow..."


2. Motivation
-------------
Not too sure about this part, but I would focus on 2 things:

- Significance of SAX as a proof system. Computational interpretation:
  shared memory concurrency but also as an intermediate representation for an
  imperative language that allows you to reason about low-level behaviour such
  as pointers manipulation and so on. Subformula property also allows us to
  reason about heap states statically and to apply several compiler
  optimizations. Can also discuss adjoint logic and message-passing.

- Importance of mechanization. Limitations of on paper proofs and why this is
  even more so apparent in this paper: Lots of proofs are not explicitly written
  down, a lot is left implicit (connect the implicit part with eligibility and
  irrelevance). Use this as an opportunity to extend on eligibility and
  irrelevance, show some examples from the paper. Discuss some of the
  complexities encountered during the mechanization related to the points made.

Most important examples to include here: transitivity proof where we can't
eliminate the cut on B, its corresponding derivation with a snip to show how
eligibility is propagated, and the principal case for implication where
elibility is lost (main argument as to why tracking eligibility adds a lot of
complexity to the cut admissibility proof).


3. Contributions
----------------
You know, what we'll deliver:

- State explicitly the proof-theoretic results that we prove (translation
  between G3 and SAX, cut admissibility, cut-elimination, subformula property)

- How we encode SAX in Beluga: LF representation of rules, schemas, and so
  on. Explain our design choices, why we chose to represent eligibility this
  way, why we don't track irrelevance.

- Translation between G3 and SAX

- Cut admissibility and cut elimination. Give an actual on paper proof and show
  the corresponding implementation. Best case is again the principal case for
  implication.

- Subformula property.
