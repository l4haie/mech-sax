\subsection{The Semi-Axiomatic Sequent Calculus}

The rules of SAX, where the noninvertible rules have been replaced by axioms and
the invertible rules are inherited from $G_3$, are given in
Figure~\ref{fig:sax-rules}.

\begin{figure}[ht!]
\[
  \infer[\mathsf{Id}_A]{\Gamma, A \vdash A}{}
  \qquad
  \infer[\mathsf{Cut}_A]{\Gamma \vdash C}{\Gamma \vdash A & \Gamma, A \vdash C}
\]
\[
  \infer[{\supset}R]{\Gamma \vdash A \supset B}{\Gamma, A \vdash B}
  \qquad
  \infer[{\supset}L^0]{\Gamma, A, A \supset B \vdash B}{}
\]
\[
  \infer[{\otimes}R^0]{\Gamma, A, B \vdash A \otimes B}{}
  \qquad
  \infer[{\otimes}L]{\Gamma, A \otimes B \vdash C}{\Gamma, A \otimes B, A, B \vdash C}
\]
\[
  \infer[\mathbf{1}R^0]{\Gamma \vdash \mathbf{1}}{}
  \qquad
  \infer[\mathbf{1}L]{\Gamma, \mathbf{1} \vdash C}{\Gamma, \mathbf{1} \vdash C}
\]
\[
  \infer[{\with}R]{\Gamma \vdash A \mathbin{\with} B}{\Gamma \vdash A & \Gamma \vdash B}
  \qquad
  \infer[{\with}L^0_1]{\Gamma, A \mathbin{\with} B \vdash A}{}
  \qquad
  \infer[{\with}L^0_2]{\Gamma, A \mathbin{\with} B \vdash B}{}

  \qquad

\label{fig:sax-rules}
\end{figure}

As discussed in the introduction, standard cut elimination fails because the
axioms introduce formulas that cannot be unpacked. Consider the following proof,
where the cut on $B$ cannot be eliminated:
\[
  \infer[{\supset}R]{A \supset B, B \supset C \vdash A \supset C}{
    \infer[\text{Cut}_B]{A, A \supset B, B \supset C \vdash C}{
      \infer[{\supset}L^0]{A, A \supset B \vdash B}{} &
      \infer[{\supset}L^0]{B, B \supset C \vdash C}{}
    }
  }
\]

However, the cut is analytic: $B$ is a subformula of the conclusion sequent, and
thus the proof exhibits the subformula property. SAX therefore restricts to such
analytic cuts, called snips, which require that the cut formula in at least one
of the premises originates from an axiom as a subformula of its principal
formula. With their addition, SAX satisfies the subformula
property~\cite{DeYoung2020}.

In order to track the origins of each formula,~\cite{DeYoung2020} introduces a
labelling system, marking formulas $y^*: A$ as eligible for a snip. We can then
propagate this through the proof by marking a variable as eligible in the
conclusion if it is eligible in at least one of its
premises~\cite{DeYoung2020}. Since the application of axioms can contain
antecedents that are irrelevant (antecedents that are not strictly needed for
the application of said axiom) we must also track these irrelevant formulas by
marking them $x^0: B$. We mark a variable as irrelevant in the conclusion if it
is irrelevant in both of its premises~\cite{DeYoung2020}. Note that eligibility
and irrelevance are properties of formula occurrences in a derivation, not part
of the syntax of sequents. The corresponding labeled proof from above, where
$t^*: B$ is eligible because it is a subformula of the principal formula of
(both) $\supset L^0$ axioms, is given below:
\[
\infer[\text{$\supset$R}]{x : A \supset B,\; y : B \supset C \vdash z : A \supset C}{
  \infer[\text{Snip}_B]{u^* : A,\; x : A \supset B,\; y : B \supset C \vdash w^* : C}{
    \infer[\supset L^0]{u^* : A,\; x : A \supset B,\; y^0 : B \supset C \vdash t^* : B}{} &
    \infer[\supset L^0]{u^0 : A,\; x^0 : A \supset B,\; t^* : B,\; y : B \supset C \vdash w^* : C}{}
  }
}
\]

Figure~\ref{fig:sax-labelled} gives the labeled rules for Soprillo, the subset
of SAX that we mechanize, covering identity ($\texttt{id}$), implication
(${\supset}R$, ${\supset}L^0$), tensor (${\otimes}R^0$, ${\otimes}L$), and
snips ($\texttt{snip}^1$, $\texttt{snip}^2$).

\subsection{Computational Interpretation of SAX}

Beyond its proof-theoretic results, SAX admits a natural operational
interpretation as a system of concurrent processes that interact
asynchronously. This interpretation has been studied across several
computational models, from shared memory concurrency~\cite{DeYoung2020}
to message-passing concurrency~\cite{FrancalanzaTP24}, and each of these interpretations has been shown to satisfy desirable properties such as progress, preservation confluence, and termination. Specifically, under the shared memory interpretation, variables are substituted by addresses at runtime, and thus the sequent
\[
  x_1 : A_1, \ldots, x_n : A_n \vdash P :: (z : C)
\]
should be understood as typing a process $P$ that reads from addresses $x_1,
\ldots, x_n$ and writes to address $z$. The operational behaviour of processes
is then determined by the rules of the proof system, for instance cut and
snip allocates a fresh memory cell, spawning a writer process and a reader
process that synchronize through it, and process expressions assigned to right
rules write to memory while those assigned to left rules read from it.

This low-level interpretation of SAX further gives rise, in extensions of SAX,
to type-theoretically justified compiler optimizations such as in-place memory
reuse~\cite{DeYoungP23}. Extensions of SAX in adjoint logic have also served as the logical foundation for concurrent language implementations~\cite{DeYoungP23}. All of these applications ultimately depend on the soundness of SAX's proof theory.

\subsection{Mechanization}

This proof theory is, however, not trivial to prove. The original paper
establishes the key proof-theoretic results, i.e.\ the admissibility of cut,
cut-elimination, and the subformula property, but leaves a
significant portion of the proofs implicit, due to their complexity. The central
difficulty, as discussed above, is the proper representation of eligibility and reasoning about it within a proof system. We hope that this project can serve as a case study in the techniques established in~\cite{crary2009substructural} and~\cite{Sanomech}.
